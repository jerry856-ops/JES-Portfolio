White walls, ancient stone, and a sky so blue it makes everything else look like it's trying too hard.

Visual Essay — Greece

"Greece doesn't let you photograph it quietly. The light insists. The color insists. You either surrender to it or you fight it and lose."

I wasn't prepared for how physical the light would feel. It's not just bright — it has weight and direction and an opinion. The white walls of Santorini don't just reflect it; they amplify it until every shadow becomes a kind of blue you haven't seen before. I shot more in three days here than in three weeks anywhere else.

"The shadow of a door frame in Santorini at noon is the most precise blue I have ever seen. I took twelve frames of it. I kept all twelve."

Greece has been photographed so many times that it's almost impossible to find an angle that hasn't been done. The challenge wasn't finding something beautiful — it was finding something true. So I looked for the heat, the dust, the tourist-free moments just after sunrise when the cats own the streets and the light is still deciding what it wants to be.

The Acropolis at 7am, with barely anyone there, rewired something in me. You don't fully understand scale in photographs. You have to stand in front of something 2,500 years old to understand what patience really means.
